Summary of the Project Idea:
The Problem
In India we grew up seeing beautiful colourful vibrant rangoli’s, right outside during  every festivals in several home. Specially in our Villages we would see our relatives make Rangoli’s on a daily basis. When our Grandma’s & mothers used to  make rangoli during festivals, we always tried to help them. But often lost patience to do such a fine art, it was very time consuming & challenging indeed!! 
But those beautiful colours, patterns and gave us such a good feeling. And we also learnt that Rangoli’s are  also considered  a part of good luck, positive energies, and to have ants and small insects eat it outside our home and don’t enter our homes!! All this made us wonder why we could not make it daily outside our homes, just like in many of our Villages? 
That’s how we came up with idea of a Rangoli RoboArt Squad!!  We chose this project because we want to make sure we don’t lose this beautiful traditional art, that also serves an ecological function.  Just because we can’t do it or get busy!! 
The Robotic Solution
Our robot is an XY plotter built with 3D-printed parts and a microcontroller. It works like this:
•	The Robot's Job: It draws the base design on the floor with perfect precision, saving us from the tiring work.
•	The User's Job: You feed the Rangoli powder into the machine and use an app to pick/draw a design.
•	Smart Features: The robot’s app has AI that suggests cool ideas for the user to implement, such as adding diyas or flowers, to make your design look even better.
Real-Life Impact
If people start using our robot, making beautiful Rangoli’s and enjoying this art form, can become a daily habit, instead of a rare almost dying festive task. And families can keep the tradition alive at a reasonable cost.

This project is about reviving an almost fizzling part of Indian culture. It uses technology to fix the "intricate, complex artistic hard work", so that people enjoy the beautiful art in their homes daily and stay engaged with their culture. Investigating Ideas for Rangoli Maker
Before settling on the final design, we explored several different robotic concepts to make Rangoli creation easier:
•	Robotic Arm: We tested using a robotic arm to hold the Rangoli nozzle. It wasn’t precise & was too expensive and complex to balance the powder flow while moving.
•	Stencil-Based Robot: We considered a machine that simply moves stencils around. It was simple to build, but it was very limited because you could only create the few designs the stencils allowed.
•	Trailing Robot: We also considered a robot that drags a nozzle like a pen. It’s okay for simple lines, but it can’t make detailed patterns. The wheels will keep smudging the rangoli, and even a tiny mistake or a wheel slip means the whole design is ruined.

We chose the XY Plotter because it was the best option. It is accurate, reliable, and easy to use, letting us draw very detailed patterns on the ground perfectly every time.

Our Difference
Our solution, the Automatic Rangoli Maker Robot, bridges the gap between traditional Indian art and modern technology. Unlike limited, single-color alternatives, our robot will feature a multi-colour dispensing system that brings vibrant life to every pattern. By integrating an AI-powered design suggester, it doesn't just execute a path; it offers creative suggestions to enhance designs with intricate elements like flowers or lamps (diyas). This system not only eliminates the manual effort and skill barriers of traditional Rangoli making but also empowers users to preserve their cultural heritage with professional-quality results on any surface.

Mechanical Construction
Our robot is an Arduino powered XY plotter. It uses two stepper motors to move in two directions: x & y. The frame is a square made of four 2-foot aluminium bars, connected by custom 3D-printed corners. Each motor is attached to a leadscrew, which turns the motor's spinning motion into linear movement. To keep the parts moving smoothly, we added steel guide rods. These rods help the parts slide in a straight line while holding them in the other axis or movements.
The powder nozzle is attached to the y-axis bar and is shaped like a funnel. A stepper motor controls a gate on the nozzle, which opens and closes to drop the powder. The whole Robot is powered by a mini SMPS which can directly be plugged into the socket.

Key sub-systems of our rangoli maker:
1.	Movement & Positioning - what draws the rangoli.
2.	Colour Dispenser - what dispenses the colour for the rangoli.
3.	Power Supply - what powers the whole system.
Movement & Positioning Mechanism
The Movement and positioning is possible by our XY plotter which is run by an Arduino. Two stepper motors move the directions robot left, right, forward, and backward. 
To make sure it moves accurately, each motor is connected to a lead screw that turns spinning into straight-line movement.

To keep the robot steady and prevent it from wobbling while drawing, we added two steel rods. These rods help the moving parts slide smoothly and stay in place. Everything is held inside a strong 2ft by 2ft square frame made of aluminium bars and 3D-printed corners. This solid build lets the robot draw perfect Rangoli patterns on any flat floor.

The Power Supply:
To ensure stable operation of the high-torque stepper motors and the control electronics, we utilize a dedicated Switched-Mode Power Supply (SMPS) unit. This unit provides a constant output of 12V and 10A, delivering a total power capacity of 120W to handle the peak current demands of the XY plotter mechanism. The SMPS is connected directly to a standard AC plug-point or socket, converting mains electricity into the regulated DC power required by the Arduino and CNC shield. This power solution prevents voltage drops that could lead to motor stalling or precision errors during the intricate Rangoli drawing process.

Software Code
Our software uses a structured workflow to translate artistic patterns into physical movement:

1.	User Interface: The laptop app interface is built with Python using the “tkinter” library. It allows users to draw patterns, select saved designs, or generate AI suggestions to collaborate as a designer using the OpenAI API. We also can import an image from the web and convert that into GCODE as well using edge & colour detection using the “matlab.engine” package.




       



2.	Path Conversion: The above app converts selected designs into GCODE. GCODE is a specialized language that provides precise coordinates and instructions for our rangoli maker.

3.	Data Transmission: Once generated, the GCODE is sent from the control device to the robot using Serial communication protocols over a wired USB link using a package called “pyserial”.

4.	Firmware Motion Control: The robot runs GRBL firmware on the Arduino. This firmware acts as an interpreter, reading the incoming GCODE and converting it into precise electrical signals (pulses) that command the stepper motors to move the nozzle accurately along the X and axes.
 
     
Use of MATLAB in the Automatic Rangoli Maker Robot
In the Automatic Rangoli Maker Robot, MATLAB plays an important role in converting IMPORTED images from the internet of rangoli designs into machine-readable coordinate paths. Since “tkinter” cannot understand images directly, MATLAB is used to process the selected design and generate the X-Y coordinates needed for drawing.
The rangoli image wa first imported into MATLAB. The software then processes the image using image processing techniques such as grayscale conversion, binary conversion, and boundary detection. After detecting the outline of the rangoli pattern, MATLAB extracts the X and Y coordinate points from the design for an IMPORTED IMAGE if you’re uploading from the web.
These coordinate points represent the exact path that the robot must follow while drawing the rangoli. The generated coordinates are then transferred to the controller or microcontroller, which controls the movement of the motors. As the motors move according to the coordinates, the robot creates the rangoli pattern automatically on the floor.
Advantages of using MATLAB in this project
Using MATLAB in the Automatic Rangoli Maker Robot provides several advantages:
•	Improves drawing accuracy and precision
•	Converts complex rangoli patterns into drawable paths
•	Reduces manual calculation effort
•	Helps generate smooth and continuous robot movement

•	Supports image processing and coordinate generation in a single platform

Challenges
Technical Challenges
1.	Software Communication and Command Execution: We struggled to turn digital designs into motor movements. We solved this by using GRBL, an open-source firmware. It lets us send G-code instructions to the Arduino, which gives us the precision needed for complex patterns.
2.	Structural Stability and Axis Alignment: The Y-axis bar was wobbly when attached to the X-axis, leading to shaky designs. We fixed this by making the robot smaller and adding reinforcements to the X-Y inter-axis connector for smoother movement.
3.	Powder Flow Regulation: The nozzle released too much powder, making lines too thick. We solved this by shrinking the nozzle diameter, which allows for finer, more precise powder control.
4.	Actuator Compatibility: Our original servo motor for the nozzle didn't work with our GRBL firmware due to compatibility issues. We replaced it with a Z-axis stepper motor, which syncs perfectly with the X and Y axes and our control software.
